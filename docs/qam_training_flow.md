# QAM Offline-to-Online Training Flow

This note summarizes the training flow of **Q-learning with Adjoint Matching
(QAM)** following the formulas in the paper.  The central implementation point is:

> The forward trajectory used for actor optimization is sampled with the
> fine-tuned policy \(f_\theta\), while the reverse adjoint ODE uses the behavior
> flow \(f_\beta\).  Therefore QAM avoids backpropagating through the full
> multi-step denoising chain of \(f_\theta\); it only needs VJPs through
> \(f_\beta\) when computing lean adjoint states.

## 1. Behavior-Constrained Policy Objective

QAM starts from a behavior-constrained policy optimization problem:

\[
\arg\max_\pi\ \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}[Q(s,a)]
\quad
\text{s.t.}
\quad
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi \Vert \pi_\beta) \le \epsilon(s).
\tag{15}
\]

For a suitable inverse temperature \(\tau(s)\), the optimal policy has the form:

\[
\pi^\star(a \mid s)
\propto
\pi_\beta(a \mid s)e^{\tau(s)Q_\phi(s,a)}.
\tag{16}
\]

Here:

- \(\pi_\beta\) is the behavior prior learned from data.
- \(Q_\phi(s,a)\) is the critic.
- \(\tau\) controls how strongly the policy is pulled toward high-Q actions.

This means QAM does not learn an unconstrained policy.  It learns a Q-weighted
tilt of the behavior distribution.

## 2. Behavior Flow \(f_\beta\)

The behavior policy is represented by a flow-matching velocity field:

\[
f_\beta : \mathcal{S} \times \mathbb{R}^A \times [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}^A.
\]

It is trained with the standard flow-matching objective:

\[
L_{\mathrm{FM}}(\beta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim D,\ t\sim[0,1],\ z\sim\mathcal{N}}
\left[
\left\|
f_\beta(s,(1-t)z+ta,t)-a+z
\right\|_2^2
\right].
\tag{17}
\]

Intuitively, \(f_\beta\) models the behavior/action distribution in the offline
data.  In QAM it also serves as the stable reference flow used to propagate the
critic gradient backward in time.

## 3. Fine-Tuned Flow \(f_\theta\) and SOC Objective

QAM parameterizes the improved policy with another flow velocity field:

\[
f_\theta : \mathcal{S} \times \mathbb{R}^A \times [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}^A.
\]

The corresponding stochastic optimal control objective is:

\[
L(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{s\sim D,\ a_t}
\left[
\int_0^1
\frac{2}{\sigma_t^2}
\left\|
f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-f_\beta(s,a_t,t)
\right\|_2^2
-
\tau(s)Q_\phi(s,a_1)
\ dt
\right].
\tag{18}
\]

The term

\[
\left\|
f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-f_\beta(s,a_t,t)
\right\|_2^2
\]

keeps \(f_\theta\) close to the behavior flow, while

\[
-\tau(s)Q_\phi(s,a_1)
\]

encourages high-value terminal actions.

Directly optimizing this objective requires backpropagation through the full
stochastic denoising process, which can be unstable.

## 4. Forward SDE Uses \(f_\theta\)

The trajectory \(\{a_t\}_{t\in[0,1]}\) in the SOC objective is defined by the
memory-less SDE:

\[
da_t
=
\left(
2f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}
\right)dt
+
\sqrt{\frac{2(1-t)}{t}}\,dB_t.
\tag{19}
\]

Define the \(f_\theta\)-drift:

\[
b_\theta(s,a_t,t)
=
2f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}.
\]

Then Equation (19) can be written as:

\[
da_t = b_\theta(s,a_t,t)dt + \sigma_t dB_t,
\quad
\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{2(1-t)}{t}}.
\]

The key point is:

> The forward trajectory for actor optimization is generated by the current
> fine-tuned actor \(f_\theta\), not by \(f_\beta\).

The paper states that this SOC problem has optimum:

\[
\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s)
\propto
\pi_\beta(\cdot \mid s)e^{\tau(s)Q_\phi(s,a)}.
\tag{20}
\]

So \(f_\theta\) should become a Q-weighted tilt of the behavior flow.

## 5. Adjoint Matching Objective

Instead of directly backpropagating through Equation (18), QAM uses the adjoint
matching objective:

\[
L_{\mathrm{AM}}(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{s\sim D,\ \{a_t\}_t}
\left[
\int_0^1
\left\|
\frac{2(f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-f_\beta(s,a_t,t))}{\sigma_t}
+
\sigma_t\tilde{g}_t
\right\|_2^2
dt
\right].
\tag{21}
\]

Here \(\tilde{g}_t\) is the **lean adjoint state**.  It turns the terminal critic
gradient at \(a_1\) into step-wise guidance for every intermediate denoising
state \(a_t\).

The loss pushes the velocity difference

\[
f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-f_\beta(s,a_t,t)
\]

to match the direction implied by \(\tilde{g}_t\).  Thus \(f_\theta\) learns how
to deviate from the behavior flow toward high-Q actions.

## 6. Reverse Adjoint ODE Uses \(f_\beta\)

The lean adjoint is defined by the reverse ODE:

\[
d\tilde{g}_t
=
-
\nabla_{a_t}
\left[
2f_\beta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}
\right]
\tilde{g}_t\,dt.
\tag{22}
\]

Define the behavior drift:

\[
b_\beta(s,a_t,t)
=
2f_\beta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}.
\]

Then:

\[
d\tilde{g}_t
=
-
\nabla_{a_t} b_\beta(s,a_t,t)\ \tilde{g}_t\,dt.
\]

This is the most important distinction in QAM:

> The trajectory \(\{a_t\}\) is sampled with \(f_\theta\), but the adjoint
> propagation uses the Jacobian of \(f_\beta\), not the Jacobian of \(f_\theta\).

Therefore QAM avoids repeatedly multiplying through

\[
\frac{\partial f_\theta(s,a_t,t)}{\partial a_t}
\]

across the whole denoising trajectory.  This is the unstable operation that
direct backpropagation through the policy would require.

QAM still uses a Jacobian-vector product, but it is taken through the behavior
flow:

\[
\left[
\frac{\partial b_\beta(s,a_t,t)}{\partial a_t}
\right]^\top
\tilde{g}_t.
\]

In implementation this should be computed as a VJP, not by materializing a full
Jacobian matrix.

## 7. Critic TD Objective

QAM combines the actor update with standard TD learning:

\[
L(\phi)
=
\mathbb{E}_{s,a,s',r\sim D}
\left[
\left(
Q_\phi(s,a)
-
r
-
\gamma Q_{\bar{\phi}}(s',a')
\right)^2
\right],
\quad
a' \leftarrow \mathrm{ODE}(f_\theta(s',\cdot,\cdot), a'_0\sim\mathcal{N}).
\tag{23}
\]

Here:

- \(Q_{\bar{\phi}}\) is the target critic.
- \(a'\) is sampled from the current fine-tuned policy \(f_\theta\).
- The paper writes this target action sampling with an ODE sampler.

For online interaction, the executed action also comes from the current
fine-tuned policy:

\[
a \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s).
\]

Thus online data collection uses \(f_\theta\), not \(f_\beta\).  The behavior
flow remains the prior/reference model used by the QAM actor objective.

## 8. Discrete Forward SDE

In practice, the paper discretizes both the forward SDE and reverse adjoint ODE
with step size:

\[
h = \frac{1}{T}.
\]

For \(a_0 \sim \mathcal{N}\) and \(z_t \sim \mathcal{N}\), the forward SDE is:

\[
a_{t+h}
\leftarrow
a_t
+
h\cdot
\left(
2f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}
\right)
+
\sqrt{\frac{2h(1-t)}{t}}z_t.
\tag{24}
\]

This creates the actor-training trajectory:

\[
\{a_0,a_h,a_{2h},\ldots,a_1\}
\leftarrow
\mathrm{SDE}(f_\theta).
\]

Again, this trajectory is generated by the current actor \(f_\theta\).

## 9. Terminal Adjoint Boundary Condition

At the final action \(a_1\), QAM computes the critic action gradient:

\[
\nabla_{a_1}Q_\phi(s,a_1).
\]

The terminal lean adjoint is:

\[
\tilde{g}_1
=
-\tau\nabla_{a_1}Q_\phi(s,a_1).
\]

This is the boundary condition for the reverse adjoint calculation.

In code, if the implementation uses \(\lambda = 1/\tau\), this appears as:

```python
adjs[-1] = -Q_grad_at_1 / lambda_t
```

where:

```python
Q_grad_at_1 = grad(Q_phi(s, a1), a1)
```

## 10. Discrete Reverse Adjoint Calculation

The paper discretizes the reverse adjoint process as:

\[
\tilde{g}_{t-h}
\leftarrow
\tilde{g}_t
+
h\cdot
\mathrm{VJP}
\left(
\nabla_{a_t}
\left(
2f_\beta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}
\right),
\tilde{g}_t
\right).
\tag{25}
\]

Equivalently:

\[
\tilde{g}_{t-h}
=
\tilde{g}_t
+
h
\left[
\frac{\partial}{\partial a_t}
\left(
2f_\beta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}
\right)
\right]^\top
\tilde{g}_t.
\]

Since

\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial a_t}
\left(
2f_\beta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t}
\right)
=
2\frac{\partial f_\beta(s,a_t,t)}{\partial a_t}
-
\frac{1}{t}I,
\]

the VJP is:

\[
\left(
2J_\beta(s,a_t,t)
-
\frac{1}{t}I
\right)^\top
\tilde{g}_t,
\]

where:

\[
J_\beta(s,a_t,t)
=
\frac{\partial f_\beta(s,a_t,t)}{\partial a_t}.
\]

Important:

- QAM does use a Jacobian-vector product.
- It uses the Jacobian of \(f_\beta\), not the Jacobian of \(f_\theta\).
- The full Jacobian matrix should not be explicitly constructed.
- Autograd computes the VJP efficiently as \(J_\beta^\top \tilde{g}_t\).

## 11. Algorithm 1 Written as Training Steps

For each training update:

1. Sample a transition:

   \[
   (s,a,s',r)\sim D.
   \]

2. Update the critic with TD backup:

   \[
   L(\phi)
   =
   \left(
   Q_\phi(s,a)
   -
   r
   -
   \gamma Q_{\bar{\phi}}(s',a'\sim\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s'))
   \right)^2.
   \]

3. Generate an actor trajectory with the current \(f_\theta\):

   \[
   \{a_0,a_h,\ldots,a_1\}
   \leftarrow
   \mathrm{SDE}(f_\theta(s,\cdot,\cdot)).
   \]

4. Compute the terminal critic action gradient:

   \[
   \tilde{g}_1
   \leftarrow
   -\tau\nabla_{a_1}Q_\phi(s,a_1).
   \]

5. Compute lean adjoint states using \(f_\beta\):

   \[
   \tilde{g}_0,\tilde{g}_h,\ldots,\tilde{g}_{1-h}
   \leftarrow
   \mathrm{LeanAdj}(f_\beta(s,\cdot,\cdot),\tilde{g}_1,\{a_t\}_t).
   \]

6. Update the actor with adjoint matching:

   \[
   L_{\mathrm{AM}}(\theta)
   =
   \sum_t
   \left\|
   \frac{2(f_\theta(s,a_t,t)-f_\beta(s,a_t,t))}{\sigma_t}
   +
   \sigma_t\tilde{g}_t
   \right\|_2^2.
   \]

## 12. Offline-to-Online Setting

During pure offline training:

\[
D = D_{\mathrm{offline}}.
\]

During online fine-tuning:

\[
D = D_{\mathrm{offline}} \cup D_{\mathrm{online}}.
\]

The online loop is:

1. Use current \(\pi_\theta\) to interact with the environment.
2. Store new transitions in the online replay buffer.
3. Continue training \(Q_\phi\) and \(f_\theta\) on mixed offline and online data.

The policy executed in the environment is:

\[
a \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s).
\]

It is not the behavior policy \(\pi_\beta\).  However, because the actor loss is
regularized around \(f_\beta\), the learned \(f_\theta\) remains behavior-aware
while being tilted toward high-Q actions.

## 13. Why This Avoids the Unstable \(f_\theta\) Jacobian Chain

Naive direct optimization would require differentiating:

\[
Q_\phi(s,a_1)
\quad
\text{through}
\quad
a_0 \to a_h \to \cdots \to a_1,
\]

where every step depends on \(f_\theta\).  This creates a long product of
Jacobians involving:

\[
\frac{\partial f_\theta(s,a_t,t)}{\partial a_t}.
\]

If these Jacobians are ill-conditioned, the gradient to \(\theta\) can become
unstable.

QAM changes the computation:

- \(f_\theta\) still generates the forward SDE trajectory.
- The terminal critic gradient is still used.
- But the reverse adjoint propagation uses \(f_\beta\):

  \[
  \left[
  \frac{\partial b_\beta(s,a_t,t)}{\partial a_t}
  \right]^\top
  \tilde{g}_t.
  \]

- The actor update then uses a step-wise squared loss instead of direct
  backpropagation through the whole sampling chain.

Thus QAM does not eliminate Jacobian-vector products entirely.  It relocates
them from the changing actor \(f_\theta\) to the behavior reference \(f_\beta\),
which is the key stability advantage.

## 14. Relation to `compute_adjoint_states`

The local helper in `rlinf/algorithms/embodiment/qam.py` computes adjoint states
with the same high-level idea:

```python
adjs[-1] = -Q_grad_at_1 / lambda_t
```

sets the terminal adjoint, and:

```python
_, vjp_x = torch.autograd.functional.vjp(
    velocity_at_x,
    x_curr,
    v=adj_next,
    create_graph=False,
    strict=False,
)
adjs[step - 1] = (adj_next + h * vjp_x).detach()
```

computes:

\[
g_{t-h}
=
g_t
+
hJ_\beta(s,x_t,t)^\top g_t.
\]

This helper is an ODE-style primitive around \(f_\beta\).  Consistent with paper
Equation (25), the VJP is taken through the **complete** behavior drift

\[
2f_\beta(s,a_t,t)-\frac{a_t}{t},
\]

evaluated at the source state \(a_t = \texttt{traj[step]}\) and source time
\(t = \texttt{step}/W\); `f_beta_fn` supplies only the velocity \(f_\beta\), and
the helper adds the \(-a_t/t\) term itself.  The matching diffusion coefficient
for the actor loss is therefore \(\sigma_t = \sqrt{2(1-t)/t}\) (implemented with
the h-shift \(\sqrt{2(1-t+h)/(t+h)}\)), **not** \(\sqrt{2(1-t)}\).
